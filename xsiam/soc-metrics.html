Key Performance Indicators

Metric Definition Target World-Class Calculation
MTTD Mean Time to Detect < 24 hours < 1 hour Avg(detection_time - attack_start_time)
MTTR Mean Time to Respond < 4 hours < 30 min Avg(containment_time - alert_time)
MTTA Mean Time to Acknowledge < 15 min < 5 min Avg(acknowledge_time - alert_time)
False Positive Rate Alerts that aren't incidents < 30% < 10% FP_alerts / total_alerts × 100
Detection Coverage MITRE ATT&CK techniques detected > 60% > 80% detected_techniques / total_techniques
Analyst Efficiency Alerts handled per analyst/day 20-40 50+ closed_alerts / analyst_FTE

Metric Calculation Queries

MTTD/MTTR Calculation in XQL

# Mean Time to Detect (MTTD) - Last 30 Days # ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════ dataset = xdr_incident_raw | filter creation_time > now() - 30d | filter status = "resolved" | alter detection_delay_hours = timestamp_diff( detection_time, first_event_time, "HOUR" ) | comp avg(detection_delay_hours) as mttd_hours, percentile(detection_delay_hours, 50) as mttd_p50, percentile(detection_delay_hours, 95) as mttd_p95 | alter mttd_target = "< 24 hours" # ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════ # Mean Time to Respond (MTTR) - Last 30 Days # ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════ dataset = xdr_incident_raw | filter creation_time > now() - 30d | filter status = "resolved" | alter response_time_hours = timestamp_diff( first_response_time, creation_time, "HOUR" ) | comp avg(response_time_hours) as mttr_hours, percentile(response_time_hours, 50) as mttr_p50, percentile(response_time_hours, 95) as mttr_p95 by severity | sort severity # ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════ # False Positive Rate by Rule # ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════ dataset = xdr_alert_raw | filter creation_time > now() - 30d | comp count() as total_alerts, countif(resolution = "false_positive") as fp_alerts, countif(resolution = "true_positive") as tp_alerts by alert_name | alter fp_rate = round(multiply(divide(fp_alerts, total_alerts), 100), 1) | filter total_alerts > 10 // Only rules with sufficient volume | sort fp_rate desc | limit 20 # ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════ # Analyst Productivity - Weekly # ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════ dataset = xdr_alert_raw | filter modification_time > now() - 7d | filter status = "resolved" | comp count() as alerts_resolved by assigned_to | sort alerts_resolved desc

Interview Questions

MTTD (Mean Time to Detect) measures how quickly your SOC identifies that an attack is occurring. It's arguably the most important SOC metric because every hour of delayed detection gives attackers more time to achieve their objectives.

Measuring MTTD accurately is challenging because you need to know when an attack actually started, not just when you detected it. For incidents where you've done thorough investigation, you can calculate the delta between first malicious activity (from forensic timeline) and when your detection fired. For routine alerts, you might use proxy metrics like time from first related event to alert creation.

To improve MTTD, I focus on three areas. First, detection coverage - are you detecting the attack techniques that matter? Map your detection rules to MITRE ATT&CK and identify gaps. Second, detection sensitivity - are your thresholds appropriate? Too high and you miss attacks; too low and you drown in alerts. Third, data latency - how quickly does data reach XSIAM and get processed? Ingestion delays directly impact MTTD.

I also look at MTTD by attack type. You might have excellent MTTD for malware (minutes via endpoint detection) but poor MTTD for insider threats (days via behavioral analytics). Breaking down the metric reveals where to focus improvement efforts.

Executive and analyst audiences have fundamentally different needs from SOC metrics. Executives want to understand risk posture and resource efficiency; analysts want operational data to improve their daily work.

For executives, I focus on outcomes and trends. MTTD and MTTR trending over time - are we getting better or worse? Critical incident count by month - is our risk exposure stable? Compliance status - are we meeting regulatory requirements? Cost per incident - is our investment efficient? These metrics should fit on one page with clear red/yellow/green indicators. Executives don't need to understand XQL queries; they need confidence that security operations are effective.

For SOC analysts and team leads, I provide operational metrics they can act on. Queue depth by priority - what needs attention right now? False positive rates by rule - which detections need tuning? Individual productivity metrics - handled with care to avoid punitive use, but useful for identifying training needs or workload imbalances. Investigation time by alert type - where are analysts spending their time?

The key distinction is actionability. Executive metrics inform strategic decisions about budget, staffing, and risk tolerance. Analyst metrics inform tactical decisions about what to work on today and where to improve processes.
