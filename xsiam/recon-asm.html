Attack Surface Management (Cortex Xpanse)

Complete guide to configuring and using Cortex Xpanse for external attack surface discovery. Covers initial setup, asset discovery, finding remediation, and integration with XSIAM.

What is Attack Surface Management?

Definition

Attack Surface Management (ASM) continuously discovers and monitors your organization's internet-facing assets. It answers: "What can attackers see from the outside?"

Cortex Xpanse is Palo Alto's ASM product, integrated into XSIAM.

What Xpanse Discovers

Infrastructure

IP ranges, domains, subdomains, cloud IPs

Web Properties

Websites, APIs, login portals, dev environments

Cloud Resources

AWS, Azure, GCP public resources, S3 buckets

Certificates

SSL/TLS certs, expiring certs, weak crypto

Where to Find ASM in XSIAM Console

XSIAM Console → Attack Surface (left navigation)

ASM Sub-sections:

Attack Surface → Overview Dashboard: Asset counts, findings by severity, trends
Attack Surface → Assets All discovered assets (IPs, domains, certs)
Attack Surface → Findings Security issues discovered (exposed services, vulns)
Attack Surface → Policies Configure what generates alerts
Attack Surface → Settings Seed data (domains, IPs to monitor)

How to Set Up Xpanse

Step 1: Configure Seed Data

Attack Surface → Settings → Seed Data

What is Seed Data?

Seed data tells Xpanse what belongs to your organization. It uses this as a starting point to discover related assets.

1
Add Root Domains

Enter your organization's primary domains:

yourcompany.com
yourcompany.co.uk
acquired-company.com
2
Add IP Ranges

Enter known public IP ranges (CIDR notation):

203.0.113.0/24 (Data center 1)
198.51.100.0/24 (Data center 2)
3
Add ASN (Optional)

If you have your own ASN, add it for automatic IP discovery

4
Wait for Discovery

Initial discovery takes 24-48 hours

Xpanse will discover subdomains, related IPs, cloud resources, and more

Understanding and Managing Findings

Viewing Findings

Attack Surface → Findings

Findings are security issues discovered on your attack surface. They're categorized by severity and type.

Example Findings with Remediation

Exposed RDP Service

CRITICAL

What: Remote Desktop Protocol (port 3389) exposed to internet on 203.0.113.45

Why it's bad: RDP is frequently brute-forced. BlueKeep and other RDP vulns are common attack vectors.

Where: 203.0.113.45:3389 (server-dc01.yourcompany.com)

Remediation Steps:
  1. Immediately block port 3389 inbound at firewall
  2. Implement VPN or jump host for remote access
  3. Enable Network Level Authentication (NLA)
  4. Review logs for brute force attempts
Console Action: Findings → Select finding → Assign to → [Team/Person] → Set Due Date

Unprotected S3 Bucket

HIGH

What: AWS S3 bucket "yourcompany-marketing-assets" allows public read access

Why it's bad: Public buckets can leak sensitive data. Often contain PII, credentials, or proprietary info.

Where: s3://yourcompany-marketing-assets (AWS us-east-1)

Remediation Steps:
  1. Review bucket contents for sensitive data
  2. Enable "Block Public Access" in AWS console
  3. Review bucket policy and ACLs
  4. Enable S3 access logging

Expiring SSL Certificate

MEDIUM

What: SSL certificate for api.yourcompany.com expires in 14 days

Why it's bad: Expired certs cause outages and security warnings, impacting customer trust

Remediation Steps:
  1. Identify cert owner/team
  2. Renew certificate
  3. Consider implementing auto-renewal (Let's Encrypt, ACM)

Interview Questions

Attack Surface Management is about continuously discovering and monitoring what your organization exposes to the internet - essentially seeing your infrastructure the way an attacker would during reconnaissance. Traditional asset management relies on what IT knows about, but ASM discovers what actually exists, which often includes shadow IT, forgotten systems, and resources from acquisitions that nobody documented.

The criticality has increased dramatically because of cloud adoption and remote work. In the old days, you had a data center with known IPs and a handful of public-facing services. Now, any developer can spin up an AWS instance, any marketing team can launch a microsite, and any IT admin can open a port for remote access. The attack surface is constantly changing without centralized visibility.

I've seen ASM tools discover things that shocked IT teams - dev environments exposed with default credentials, backup servers with RDP open, S3 buckets with customer data that were "supposed to be private." Attackers actively scan for these misconfigurations. Having continuous ASM means you find these issues before attackers do, ideally within hours of them being exposed.

In XSIAM, Cortex Xpanse provides this capability integrated with the SIEM/XDR. When Xpanse finds exposed RDP, it can automatically create an incident, trigger a playbook to block the port, and notify the responsible team. This integration closes the loop between discovery and response rather than having ASM findings sit in a separate tool waiting for manual action.

Exposed RDP is one of the most critical findings because it's a direct attack vector - ransomware groups actively scan for it and either brute force credentials or exploit vulnerabilities like BlueKeep. My response is immediate and systematic.

First, I verify the finding isn't a false positive by checking if port 3389 actually responds. Then I identify the asset - is this a domain controller, database server, or standard workstation? A DC with exposed RDP is a potential domain compromise waiting to happen. I immediately create an emergency firewall rule to block 3389 from the internet while we investigate. This is a "block first, ask questions later" situation because the risk is too high.

While the block is being implemented, I check for compromise indicators. I review authentication logs for brute force attempts - look for Event IDs 4625 (failed logons) from external IPs. I check for successful unknown logons, especially SYSTEM or administrator accounts. I look at process creation events for anything suspicious launched during RDP sessions. If I find evidence of successful attack, this escalates to full incident response.

For remediation, I work with the team to implement proper remote access - VPN, jump hosts, or cloud solutions like Azure Bastion. I ensure NLA is enabled and the system is patched against RDP vulnerabilities. Then I add a correlation rule to detect any future RDP brute force attempts, because even if we close this hole, attackers might find another RDP endpoint. Finally, I create a post-incident report documenting how this got exposed and recommendations to prevent recurrence.

When facing a large backlog of ASM findings, I use a risk-based prioritization that considers both the severity of the exposure and the value of the asset. Severity alone isn't enough - an exposed test server might be technically critical but doesn't warrant the same urgency as an exposed production database.

I create tiers for remediation. Tier 1 is "drop everything" - exposed remote access services (RDP, SSH, VNC) on any system, plus any data exposure containing PII or credentials. These get same-day attention. Tier 2 is "this week" - high severity findings on production assets, vulnerable public-facing web services, and public cloud storage without sensitive data. Tier 3 is "this month" - expiring certificates, outdated software versions, informational findings that don't pose immediate risk.

I also group findings by owner. Instead of creating 50 individual tickets, I'll create one ticket per team with all their findings listed and prioritized. This is more actionable and creates accountability at the team level. I track remediation metrics - mean time to remediate by severity, teams with open critical findings, and trend over time. These metrics get reported to leadership to create organizational pressure for timely fixes.

The key is continuous triage as new findings come in. I have Xpanse configured to auto-create incidents for critical findings so they get immediate attention, while lower severity findings flow into a weekly review queue. This prevents critical issues from getting lost in the noise of hundreds of informational findings.

ASM and vulnerability management are related but distinct disciplines. Vulnerability management typically starts with known assets - you scan servers and workstations that are in your asset inventory using authenticated scanners like Qualys or Nessus. ASM starts from the internet perspective - it discovers what exists and what's exposed without needing authentication or an existing inventory.

The key difference is scope. Vulnerability management finds CVEs on assets you know about. ASM finds assets you didn't know about in the first place. I've seen environments where vulnerability management shows green - all known systems patched and hardened - but ASM reveals forgotten development servers, subsidiary infrastructure, and cloud resources that were never in the vulnerability scanning scope.

They complement each other powerfully. ASM provides discovery that feeds into vulnerability management - newly discovered assets should be added to vulnerability scanning scope. Vulnerability management provides depth that ASM lacks - while ASM might identify an Apache server exists, vulnerability scanning tells you the specific version and CVEs present. I think of ASM as answering "what's exposed?" and vulnerability management answering "what's vulnerable on those exposures?"

In mature programs, I integrate both. When Xpanse discovers a new subdomain, it triggers a workflow to add that asset to our Qualys scanning scope and verify it has an endpoint agent. This closes the gap between external discovery and internal security controls. The goal is zero unmanaged external assets - everything exposed should be inventoried, scanned, and monitored.

Setting up ASM from scratch requires careful planning because the initial discovery often reveals an overwhelming number of findings. I approach it as a phased implementation to avoid paralyzing teams with hundreds of issues simultaneously.

Phase one is seed data collection. I work with IT, networking, cloud teams, and M&A to compile all known domains, IP ranges, cloud accounts, and ASNs. This often reveals knowledge gaps immediately - different teams have different lists, nobody has a complete picture. I also review certificate transparency logs and DNS records to find domains that might have been forgotten. This seed data feeds into Xpanse to ensure comprehensive discovery.

Phase two is initial discovery with silent alerting. I run Xpanse for 1-2 weeks without creating incidents, just collecting data. This gives me a baseline of the attack surface and existing issues. I categorize findings, identify quick wins (obviously exposed RDP that can be blocked immediately), and identify asset owners for major findings. This prep work makes the remediation phase manageable.

Phase three is remediation kickoff. I present findings to leadership with clear prioritization - critical issues first, then high, etc. I work with teams to set realistic SLAs based on organizational capacity. For organizations with hundreds of critical findings, I might focus the first month just on tier 1 (remote access exposure) rather than trying to boil the ocean. As critical issues get resolved, we work down the severity stack.

Phase four is operationalization. Once the initial backlog is under control, I enable automated incident creation for new findings and integrate ASM into regular security operations. New critical findings get immediate response, weekly triage handles lower severity issues, and monthly reporting tracks the attack surface trend over time. The goal is to reach steady state where new exposures are identified and remediated faster than they accumulate.
