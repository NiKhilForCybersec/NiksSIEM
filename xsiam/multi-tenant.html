Interview Questions

For an MSSP with 50 customers, I'd recommend separate tenants for most customers with a centralized management approach. The key considerations are data isolation, operational efficiency, and scalability.

First, I'd segment customers by requirements. Large enterprise customers or those in regulated industries (healthcare, finance) absolutely need their own tenants - complete data isolation is non-negotiable. Smaller SMB customers might be grouped into shared tenants with permission group isolation if they're comfortable with logical rather than physical separation and cost is a factor.

For the management layer, I'd implement a "single pane of glass" approach using XSIAM's Management Hub combined with custom API integrations. The MSSP SOC needs to see alerts across all tenants, track SLAs, and manage escalations without logging into 50 different consoles. Build dashboards that aggregate key metrics from all tenants and filter by customer for drill-down.

Standardization is critical at scale. Create content packs with your standard detection rules, playbooks, and dashboards. Deploy these across all tenants consistently. Use version control and change management - when you update a detection rule, you need to propagate it to all tenants systematically, not ad-hoc.

For customer-specific customizations, maintain documentation of what's unique per customer. Some customers will have custom rules for their specific applications or compliance requirements. Track these so they don't get overwritten during standard content updates.

Permission groups within a single tenant make sense when you need logical separation but benefit from shared infrastructure and cross-domain detection. The trade-off is between isolation strength and operational simplicity.

I'd choose permission groups for internal enterprise scenarios - business units within the same company that need to see only their data but don't have regulatory requirements for complete isolation. A global company might have Americas, EMEA, and APAC teams that primarily work their regional alerts, but you still want detection rules that can correlate an attack spanning regions. Permission groups give you that - the rules see all data, users see filtered results.

I'd also use permission groups for cost-sensitive MSSP scenarios with SMB customers who don't require strict isolation. Running 50 separate tenants is operationally expensive; consolidating into a few shared tenants with careful permission group design can reduce management overhead significantly.

I'd avoid permission groups when there's any regulatory requirement for data isolation (GDPR, HIPAA, financial regulations), when customers are competitors who shouldn't share any infrastructure, when licensing models require per-customer metering that's easier with separate tenants, or when customers want self-service administration capabilities that require their own tenant.
