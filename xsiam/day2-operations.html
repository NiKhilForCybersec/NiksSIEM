Day-2 Operations Runbook

Comprehensive operational procedures for maintaining XSIAM after initial deployment. Covers daily, weekly, monthly, and quarterly tasks for SOC operations, platform maintenance, and continuous improvement.

Day-2 vs Day-1

Day-1 is deployment and initial configuration. Day-2 is everything after - ongoing operations, maintenance, optimization, and continuous improvement. Most SIEM implementations fail not in deployment but in Day-2 operations.

Interview Questions

Effective handover is critical - the next shift needs to hit the ground running, not spend their first hour figuring out what happened.

My handover includes:

Shift statistics: How many alerts came in, how many were handled, any incidents created or closed. This gives the next shift a sense of whether it was a normal day or if something unusual is happening.

Open work: Any investigations in progress, their current status, and what the next steps are. If I was in the middle of investigating suspicious PowerShell activity, the next analyst needs to know where I left off.

Notable events: Anything significant that happened, even if it's closed. If there was a true positive ransomware detection that we contained, the next shift should know in case related activity appears.

Platform issues: Any problems with XSIAM itself - data sources that stopped flowing, performance issues, integrations that broke. The next shift shouldn't be surprised by known issues.

Pending actions: Tasks that need follow-up - tuning requests submitted, escalations awaiting response, scheduled maintenance coming up.

I document all this in a standard template and also do a verbal handover for anything complex. Good handovers prevent context loss that leads to missed threats.

I track metrics across three dimensions: detection effectiveness, operational efficiency, and coverage.

Detection effectiveness: True positive rate and false positive rate are the core metrics. If we're generating alerts, are they finding real threats? I also track mean time to detect (MTTD) - how long from when malicious activity occurs until we alert on it. For incidents, I track whether we detected the threat or if it was reported to us.

Operational efficiency: Mean time to respond (MTTR) measures how long from alert to resolution. Alert volume per analyst shows if we're at capacity. SLA compliance tracks whether we're meeting response time commitments. I also look at automation rate - what percentage of alerts are handled without human intervention?

Coverage: What percentage of our environment is actually monitored? Agent deployment rate, data source coverage, MITRE ATT&CK technique coverage. These show whether we'd even see an attack if it happened.

What I avoid: Vanity metrics like "total alerts blocked" or "attacks prevented" that sound impressive but don't indicate security posture. I also don't obsess over alert counts - a quiet day isn't bad if coverage is maintained.

Trending matters: Individual numbers matter less than trends. If MTTR is increasing over time, that's a problem even if we're still meeting SLAs. If FP rate is decreasing, our tuning efforts are working.
